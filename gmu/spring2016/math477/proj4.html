The Brusselator Model

For Project 4, we chose to simulate Brusselator model. The Brusselator (a portmanteau of Brussels and oscillator) is an example of Turing instability. In 1952, Alan Turing published his landmark paper, "The Chemical Basis of Morphogenesis". Alan Turing systems consisting of chemical substances (which were called morphogens) to develop an understanding of topics such as skin pigmentation. These morphogens diffused through skin tissue (moving from regions of greater concentration to lower concentration) and reacted together. He modeled these as reaction diffusion equations, and he found that some patterns from nature appeared in what were homogenous, uniform states. These Turing patterns as they came to be called took on shapes such as stripes, spots, spirals, and hexagons.

Pattern Formations in the Brusselator

We began by applying the Backward Difference Method with Newton's iteration to the Brusselator equation with homogenous Neumann boundary conditions on the square \([0, 40] \times [0, 40]\)

\begin{align} p_t &= D_p \Delta p + p^2 q + C - (K + 1) p\\ q_t &= D_q \Delta q + p^2 q + K p\\~\\ \end{align} \begin{align} \begin{cases} p(x,y,0) &= C + 0.1 &\text{for } 0 \leq x, y \leq 40\\ q(x,y,0) &= \frac{K}{C} + 0.2 &\text{for } 0 \leq x, y \leq 40\\ u_{\vec{n}} &= 0 &\text{on rectangle boundary, for all } t \geq 0 \end{cases} \end{align}
